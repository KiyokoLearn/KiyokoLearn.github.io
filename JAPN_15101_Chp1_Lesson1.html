Lesson 1 -- The Writing System


In Japanese there are three different writing systems, Hiragana, Katakana, and Kanji. All three could be found in one sentence.
  • Hiragana is for particals and verb conjugations, which will be discussed in greater detail in later lessons. For now, most vocabulary words will be written in Hiragana.
  • Katakana is used for foreign words
  • Kanji is mostly used for nouns, adjectives, and the stems of verbs.

Unlike the English alphabet which consists of consonants and vowels, the Japanese alphabet consists of syllables.
For Example:
  • English: a, b, c, d, e
  • Japanese: ka, ki, ku, ke, ko

There are 46 main Hiragana symbols and they are considered to have a "roundish" shape. Adding things like tenten and maru, which will be discussed in later lessons, create more sounds. Contracted sounds can also be made.

Similarily, there are 46 main Katakana symbols, but they are considered to have more "straight" lines. Tenten and maru can also be added to Katakana as well as contracted sounds.

Kanji comes from the Chinese characters and were introduced in Japan before Japan had a writing system. Hiragana and Katakana are considered to be simplified Chinese characters. Kanji characters are a little more complex than Hiragana and Katakana. They can represent meanings and sounds, and there are two readings for them. The on-yomi, or Chinese readings, come from the Chinese pronunciation and may have more than one reading. The kun-yomi, or Japanese readings, were used to write native Japanese words.
There are four basic types of Kanji:

  • Picture Based
  • Simple Ideograms
  • Compound Ideograms
  • Phonetic Ideographic Characters
